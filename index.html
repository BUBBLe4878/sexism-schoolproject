Understanding the Problem

What Is Sexism?

Sexism is prejudice, stereotyping, or discrimination based on a person's sex or gender. It can be overt and obvious, or subtle and hard to name. It shows up in individual actions, cultural attitudes, and in the systems and institutions that shape our lives.

Sexism affects people of all genders, though women and girls are disproportionately impacted. It also intersects with race, class, sexuality, and disability ‚Äî meaning some people face compounded forms of discrimination.

Overt
Hostile Sexism
Explicit, often aggressive attitudes that demean or objectify based on gender. Includes harassment, derogatory comments, and outright discrimination.
Hidden
Benevolent Sexism
Seemingly "positive" stereotypes that still limit people ‚Äî like assuming women need protection or aren't suited for leadership. These feel complimentary but reinforce inequality.
Structural
Institutional Sexism
Discrimination baked into laws, policies, hiring practices, media, and cultural norms. It doesn't require individual bad intent ‚Äî it operates through systems.
Everyday
Microaggressions
Subtle, often unintentional slights or dismissals ‚Äî being talked over, having ideas credited to others, or facing different expectations based on gender.

Taking Action

How to Prevent Sexism

Change happens at every level ‚Äî from individual choices to institutional reform. Here's how you can be part of the solution.

01

Educate Yourself & Others

Learn how sexism operates at systemic and interpersonal levels. Share what you learn with your community. Understanding bias is essential before you can challenge it.

02

Challenge Stereotypes

When you hear gender-based assumptions ‚Äî in conversation, media, or policies ‚Äî name them. Saying "that's a stereotype" or asking "why?" can shift thinking.

03

Amplify Marginalized Voices

In meetings, classrooms, and public spaces, actively pass the mic. If someone's idea is ignored and then repeated by another person, credit the original speaker.

04

Support Equal Pay & Policy

Advocate for pay transparency at your workplace. Support political candidates and policies that advance gender equality in law and in institutions.

05

Reflect on Your Own Bias

Everyone has unconscious biases. Regular self-reflection ‚Äî noticing when you make gender-based assumptions ‚Äî is how you reduce them over time.

06

Support Organizations Fighting Sexism

Donate to or volunteer with groups working on gender equality, domestic violence prevention, women's leadership, and policy reform.

Bystander Intervention

When you witness sexism, you have power. Here's how to respond safely and effectively:

1
Distract. Change the subject or create an interruption to defuse the situation without direct confrontation.
2
Delegate. Find someone in a position of authority ‚Äî a manager, teacher, or bystander ‚Äî who can help.
3
Document. If it's safe to do so, record what's happening so there's evidence to report later.
4
Direct. Address the behavior directly and calmly: "That comment was inappropriate."
5
Check In. After the incident, ask the person targeted how they're doing. Follow up and offer support.
