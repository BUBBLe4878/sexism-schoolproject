Understanding the Problem

What Is Sexism?

Sexism is prejudice, stereotyping, or discrimination based on a person's sex or gender. It can be overt and obvious, or subtle and hard to name. It shows up in individual actions, cultural attitudes, and in the systems and institutions that shape our daily lives.

Sexism affects people of all genders, though women and girls are disproportionately impacted. It also intersects with race, class, sexuality, and disability ‚Äî meaning some people face compounded, overlapping forms of discrimination.

Overt
Hostile Sexism
Explicit, often aggressive attitudes that demean or objectify people based on gender. Includes harassment, derogatory language, and outright discrimination.
Hidden
Benevolent Sexism
Seemingly "positive" stereotypes that still limit people ‚Äî like assuming women need protection or aren't suited for leadership. These feel complimentary but reinforce inequality.
Structural
Institutional Sexism
Discrimination baked into laws, policies, hiring practices, and cultural norms. It doesn't require individual bad intent ‚Äî it operates through systems.
Everyday
Microaggressions
Subtle, often unintentional slights ‚Äî being talked over, having ideas credited to others, or being held to different standards based on gender.

Taking Action

How to Prevent Sexism

Change happens at every level ‚Äî from individual choices to institutional reform. Here's how you can be part of the solution.

01

Educate Yourself & Others

Learn how sexism operates at systemic and interpersonal levels. Share what you learn with your community. Understanding bias is essential before you can challenge it.

02

Challenge Stereotypes When You See Them

When you encounter gender-based assumptions ‚Äî in conversation, media, or policy ‚Äî name them. Asking "why?" or saying "that's a stereotype" can shift thinking more than you'd expect.

03

Amplify Marginalized Voices

In meetings and classrooms, actively pass the mic. If someone's idea is ignored and then repeated by another person, credit the original speaker out loud.

04

Advocate for Equal Pay & Policy

Support pay transparency at your workplace. Back political candidates and policies that advance gender equality in law and in institutions where you have influence.

05

Reflect on Your Own Bias

Everyone holds unconscious biases. Regular self-reflection ‚Äî noticing when you make gender-based assumptions ‚Äî is how they shrink over time. Awareness is the starting point.

06

Support Organizations Fighting Sexism

Donate to or volunteer with groups working on gender equality, domestic violence prevention, women's leadership, and policy reform at every level.

Bystander Intervention

When you witness sexism happening, you have more power than you think. Here's how to respond safely and effectively using the 5 D's:

1
Distract. Change the subject or create an interruption to defuse the situation without direct confrontation.
2
Delegate. Find someone in a position of authority ‚Äî a manager, teacher, or bystander ‚Äî who can step in and help.
3
Document. If safe, record what's happening so there's evidence to report later. Don't intervene physically.
4
Direct. Address the behavior calmly and clearly: "That comment was inappropriate and not okay."
5
Check In. After the incident, ask the person targeted how they're doing. Follow up. Offer support.
