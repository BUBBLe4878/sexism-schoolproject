Antigone & Sexism

In Sophocles' Antigone, the protagonist defies King Creon's authority to bury her brother ‚Äî directly challenging the gendered power structure of ancient Athens. Creon's rage is partly political, but deeply gendered: a woman daring to defy a man's decree.

Creon frames female authority as shameful disorder, reflecting 5th-century BCE Athens where women were excluded from civic life entirely. Antigone's defiance ‚Äî and her punishment ‚Äî mirrors real patterns of how women who challenge power are silenced.

Themes to Analyze

Consider how Antigone's defiance, Creon's response, and the tragic outcome connect to current patterns: the silencing of women who speak out, framing female autonomy as chaos, and the personal cost of resistance.

3+ Historical Periods

Period 1 ¬∑ Ancient Greece (~450 BCE)
Women as Property
Athenian women had no legal standing, couldn't own property, and were transferred from father to husband. Female voices in public life were treated as disruptions to the "natural order" ‚Äî as Antigone discovered.
Period 2 ¬∑ 1800s‚ÄìEarly 1900s
Suffrage & Legal Exclusion
Women were legally barred from voting, owning property independently, or entering most professions. The suffragist movement was a direct fight against institutionalized sexism embedded in law.
Period 3 ¬∑ Mid‚ÄìLate 20th Century
Second-Wave Feminism
Fighting workplace discrimination, reproductive rights, and domestic violence. The second wave challenged the expectation that women find fulfillment solely through homemaking and domesticity.

The Rhetorical Triangle

Aristotle's three modes of persuasion must all be present and working together ‚Äî not as separate moments, but interwoven throughout your entire presentation. The 5-point presenter uses all three naturally.

Ethos ‚Äî Credibility
Cite credible sources. Reference expert voices. Show you've done the research. Your audience must trust you before they'll be persuaded by you.
‚ù§Ô∏è
Pathos ‚Äî Emotion
Share a story, a powerfully framed statistic, or an image that makes the abstract concrete. People act from feeling, not just facts.

What Makes a Strong Proposal?

01
Specific

Name the Exact Problem

Don't propose to "end sexism." Address one specific manifestation ‚Äî gender bias in grading, pay transparency policy, bystander training, etc.

02
Feasible

Make It Realistic

Your proposal should be achievable with available resources. Who implements it? What does it cost? What's the timeline?

03
Evidence-Based

Show It Can Work

Cite examples of similar initiatives that succeeded elsewhere. Precedent makes your proposal much more credible.

04
Measurable

Define Success

How would you know if it worked? What outcomes, data points, or behavioral changes would you expect to see?
